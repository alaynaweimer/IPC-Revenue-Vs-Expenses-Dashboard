üìä Why Track Revenue vs Expenses?

Profit doesn't happen by accident. Tracking revenue against expenses each month shows you whether your business is actually making money ‚Äî not just staying busy.

This dashboard helps you spot trends before they become problems. A dip in revenue or spike in expenses is much easier to fix when you catch it in month 2, not month 12.

‚ö†Ô∏è The danger of not tracking: Many business owners feel "busy" and assume they're profitable. Then tax time comes and they realize they lost money for 6 months straight.

üìà Key Metrics Explained

Revenue

All money coming into your business ‚Äî client payments, product sales, service fees. This is your "top line" before any expenses.

Expenses

All money going out ‚Äî rent, software, contractors, supplies, marketing. Include your own salary/draw here too.

Net Profit

Revenue minus Expenses. This is what your business actually keeps. Positive = making money. Negative = losing money.

üö® Warning Signs to Watch

üìâ

Revenue Declining 3+ Months

One bad month is normal. Three in a row is a trend. Time to investigate ‚Äî lost clients? Seasonal? Market shift?

üìà

Expenses Growing Faster Than Revenue

If expenses increase 20% but revenue only grows 5%, your margins are shrinking. Cut costs or raise prices.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Profit Margin Below 10%

For service businesses, anything under 10% net margin means you're working hard but keeping very little.

üî¥

Negative Profit Months

Occasional red months happen (big purchases, slow season). But multiple negative months need immediate action.

üíº Pro Tips From the Field

  • Update weekly, review monthly: Don't wait until month-end to enter data. Quick weekly updates keep you honest and catch issues faster.
  • Categorize expenses consistently: "Marketing" should mean the same thing every month. Inconsistent categories make trends impossible to spot.
  • Include owner's draw as expense: If you're pulling $4,000/month for yourself, that's an expense. A business that only profits because you don't pay yourself isn't really profitable.
  • Compare month-over-month AND year-over-year: Some businesses are seasonal. Compare this March to last March, not just to February.
  • Set a profit target: Don't just track ‚Äî set a goal. "I want to profit $3,000 minimum each month." Then manage toward it.
  • Separate one-time from recurring: A $5,000 equipment purchase shouldn't make you panic about "high expenses." Know what's normal vs exceptional.
